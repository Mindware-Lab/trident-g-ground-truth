

## What Makes This Vision Strong

### 1. The "Not Brain Training" Positioning Is Exactly Right

The market has soured on generic brain training. Lumosity's $2 million FTC settlement for deceptive claims, the broader skepticism toward "neuroplasticity apps," and the regulatory tightening around health claims means the old model is dead. Your repeated disclaimers—**"Not diagnosis. Not therapy. Not generic brain training"**—aren't just legal protection; they're a market positioning that aligns with where payer and consumer trust actually sits in 2026.

The regulatory landscape confirms this: Medicare's new DMHT codes reimburse *clinician-supervised, regulated software-delivered treatment*—not wellness apps or engagement metrics. FDA clearance is necessary but not sufficient; reimbursement depends on clinical accountability, documented oversight, and measurable outcomes. Your "support dashboard, not diagnosis or surveillance" framing maps directly onto this emerging reimbursement logic. 

### 2. Three-Pronged Market Segmentation Is Economically Astute

You've segmented into:
- **Clinical-Adjacent** (supervised programmes, NHS/IAPT-style, ADHD support, neurorehab)
- **Cognitive Wellness** (health/wellbeing clinics, biohacking, corporate wellbeing)
- **Cognitive Performance** (operations control, corporate, education)

This matters because these three segments have *different* funding mechanisms and risk profiles:

| Segment | Funding Source | Regulatory Risk | Growth Trajectory |
|---|---|---|---|
| Clinical-Adjacent | NHS/local authority contracts, insurance reimbursement | High (MHRA/FDA medical device pathway) | Steady, evidence-dependent |
| Cognitive Wellness | Consumer-direct, employer wellness budgets, clinic subscriptions | Low-to-moderate (wellness/ lifestyle claims) | Fastest growth, but trust-sensitive |
| Cognitive Performance | Corporate L&D budgets, education procurement, SaaS | Low | Strong B2B pipeline, measurable ROI |

In the UK specifically, the MHRA's 2026 regulatory roadmap is tightening software medical device classification while creating an International Reliance Pathway for FDA-approved products. This means your clinical-adjacent route needs to either pursue FDA clearance first (then leverage IRP for UK access) or engage directly with the new UKCA framework. The vision's "not diagnosis" framing actually helps here—you may be able to stay below medical device threshold if you're careful about claims. 

### 3. The "Attention-First" Architecture Is Clinically Credible

The layered cognitive stack (Readiness → Attention Control → Working Memory → Reasoning → Transfer) mirrors actual cognitive neuroscience hierarchies. This isn't gamified n-back training; it's a structured intervention architecture. In an era where workplace wellness spending is projected to top $94.6 billion globally but outcomes are increasingly questioned, having a *theoretically coherent* training architecture—not just a collection of tasks—is a genuine differentiator. 

The corporate wellness market is specifically shifting from "participation metrics" to "performance-enabled models" with outcome-based contracts. Your Performance Infrastructure vision, with its "cognitive bottleneck diagnostics" and "team readiness indices," speaks directly to procurement teams who now demand measurable workforce impact rather than engagement curves. 

### 4. The "Supervised" and "Institute-Led" Framing Is Reimbursement-Ready

This is perhaps the most economically important design choice. Medicare's DMHT codes and NHS IAPT pathways both reimburse *clinical management and oversight*, not software licenses. By embedding your product in supervised workflows (screening → support plan → practice → follow-up → case review), you're building for the reimbursement infrastructure that actually exists, not the one you wish existed.

The NHS has already integrated digital CBT platforms like SilverCloud through IAPT. Your clinical-adjacent model could slot into similar pathways—provided you generate the evidence base and navigate NHS commissioning structures.

## The Risks and Vulnerabilities

### 1. The "Cognitive Support Layer" Is Ambiguous in Regulatory Terms

You position between "wellness" and "therapy," but regulators don't love gray zones. In the UK, MHRA's 2026 regulations explicitly prohibit "misleading or unsubstantiated promotional claims" with stronger enforcement powers. If your "attention control training" starts making implicit therapeutic claims (e.g., for ADHD support routes), you could trigger medical device classification without intending to. The vision needs a very clear regulatory map for each segment. 

### 2. Evidence Generation Will Be Expensive and Slow

The vision leans heavily on "psychometric testing," "proof layers," and "real-life transfer checks." These are expensive to validate. The German DiGA model shows that even with fast-track provisional listing, you need to demonstrate "positive health care effects" within 12 months. Your three segments may need *different* evidence strategies:
- Clinical-Adjacent: RCTs, NICE-style health technology assessment
- Wellness: Real-world evidence, user-reported outcomes, engagement metrics
- Performance: Productivity correlates, pre-post organisational metrics

This triple evidence burden is substantial for an early-stage venture.

### 3. Corporate Wellness Budgets Are Under Scrutiny

While 41% of employers plan to increase wellness spending, there's a parallel trend toward *consolidation* and *outcome-based contracting*. Generic "brain training" is exactly what procurement teams are cutting. Your Performance Infrastructure needs to demonstrate compounding operational efficiencies—not just cognitive improvements—to justify B2B pricing. The good news is your "cognitive bottleneck diagnostics" and "decision-support metrics" are designed for this; the risk is that the measurement infrastructure itself is complex to build and validate. 

### 4. The UK Market Is Structurally Harder Than the US

NHS commissioning is fragmented, NICE evaluations are slow, and post-Brexit regulatory alignment is still settling. The US has clearer (if narrower) reimbursement pathways via Medicare DMHT codes and employer wellness budgets. Your vision might find traction faster in the US corporate wellness and clinical-adjacent markets, with the UK as a longer-term NHS integration play.

## The Core Principle: Same Infrastructure, Different Trust Layers

Both Cognitive Support Labs and HRP Lab can share underlying task architectures and theoretical frameworks (the Trident G stack, attention control paradigms, the layered cognitive model). What changes is the **trust and accountability layer** above them:

| Layer | B2B (Organisational) | B2C (Individual) |
|---|---|---|
| **Data** | Aggregate dashboards, de-identified cohort analytics | Personal progress only, no benchmarking |
| **Psychometrics** | Full battery, normed, validated for organisational decision-making | Simplified self-assessment, no diagnostic framing |
| **Supervision** | Coach/clinician dashboard, case review, institutional oversight | Self-guided with optional async coaching |
| **Claims** | Performance-enablement, readiness, operational efficiency | "Cognitive fitness," "thinking skills," "focus training" |
| **Regulatory** | Medical device adjacent (careful), HR/wellness compliance | Wellness/lifestyle, consumer protection only |
| **Reimbursement** | Employer wellness budgets, NHS contracts, insurance | Direct consumer, subscription |

The key is that the **B2C product is not a "lite version" of B2B**—it's a different product category with different risk profiles, even if the underlying cognitive tasks are identical.

## Cognitive Support Labs: B2B-First with a Careful B2C Edge

### B2B Core (Primary Revenue)
- **Clinical-Adjacent**: Supervised programmes for NHS/IAPT, neurorehab clinics, ADHD support services. Full psychometric layer, dashboard for supervisors, case review workflows.
- **Corporate/Organisational**: Cognitive Performance Infrastructure. "Cognitive control surface layered over operations control surface." Bespoke integration into existing HR/workflow systems. Team readiness indices, bottleneck diagnostics.

### B2C Edge (Secondary, Simplified)
Your instinct to strip back psychometrics and dashboards is correct, but I'd push further on what *remains*:

| What to Keep | What to Strip | What to Add |
|---|---|---|
| Core attention training tasks (the actual cognitive intervention) | Norm-referenced psychometric batteries | Simple "zone check" (self-report readiness, not diagnostic) |
| Progress tracking (personal, trend-only) | Cohort analytics, benchmarking | "Cognitive coaching" as async text/check-in layer |
| Theoretical coherence (users feel the layers build) | Clinical framing, "support plan" language | Community/peer elements (lower accountability than supervision) |

**For anxiety specifically**: The B2C app should *not* be positioned as an anxiety intervention. It should be "cognitive readiness training" that happens to help with focus under pressure. The moment you target anxiety as a symptom, you cross into medical/therapeutic claims. Users with anxiety will find it; you don't need to claim it.

**The B2C pricing model matters here**: If it's subscription-based consumer wellness, the FTC/MHRA scrutiny is lower. If you offer "cognitive coaching" as a human layer, that becomes a service business with different margins and scaling constraints. Be explicit about whether coaching is:
- **Async** (scalable, lower cost, lower efficacy evidence)
- **Synchronous** (less scalable, higher cost, stronger outcomes, harder to prove)
- **Hybrid** (AI-first with human escalation—most defensible for 2026)

## HRP Lab (IQ Mindware / EQ Mindware): B2C as "Plug-In" to Systems

This is where your vision gets interesting. The individual isn't buying "brain training"—they're buying **access to a cognitive layer** that makes them more effective within systems they already inhabit.

### The B2C Value Proposition
> "Train the cognitive skills that education, work, and high-demand settings actually require. Then plug back in, sharper."

This flips the consumer pitch from "improve your brain" (discredited) to **"skill acquisition for specific contexts"** (credible, actionable).

| Context | What the Individual Gets | What the Organisation Sees (if integrated) |
|---|---|---|
| **Education** | Attention control for study, exam readiness, cognitive stamina | (Optional) Aggregate readiness for student support services |
| **Work** | Focus under pressure, decision-making clarity, recovery routines | (Optional) Team cognitive health metrics, if employer subscribes to B2B tier |
| **High-Demand Settings** (athletes, performers, clinicians) | Personal cognitive profiling, overload detection, transfer training | N/A—purely individual unless institutional contract exists |

### The "Cognitive Coaching" Tier
This is your human layer for B2C. Key design choices:

- **Positioning**: Not therapy, not tutoring. "Cognitive performance coaching"—like a strength coach, but for attention control and reasoning under pressure.
- **Credentialing**: Coaches need clear boundaries. They are *not* clinicians. Training in the Trident G framework, basic motivational interviewing, but explicit scope limits.
- **Integration with B2B**: The same coach pool can serve organisational contracts (supervised, dashboard-mediated) and individual clients ( lighter, progress-focused). This creates economies of scale and brand coherence.

### The B2B Tier for HRP Lab
More sophisticated than Cognitive Support Labs' corporate offering? Or overlapping? You need to be precise:

| | Cognitive Support Labs B2B | HRP Lab B2B |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary buyer** | HR/Operations, clinic directors, NHS commissioners | Education institutions, professional bodies, elite performance programmes |
| **Core need** | Workforce cognitive health, operational efficiency, supervised support | Individual cognitive development within institutional contexts |
| **Metrics** | Team readiness, bottleneck diagnostics, support-route patterns | Individual progress, transfer evidence, readiness for high-stakes performance |
| **Integration** | Embedded in workflow/HR systems | Embedded in learning management or performance management systems |

There's overlap here. You'll need to decide whether HRP Lab's B2B is a *premium tier* (same product, more bespoke) or a *different product* (different metrics, different integration points). My recommendation: **different integration points, shared cognitive architecture**. This avoids cannibalisation while maximising R&D leverage.

## Critical Boundary: The "Medical Device" Cliff

Both labs need a hard internal rule:

> **No B2C product makes claims about diagnosing, treating, or preventing any medical condition.**

This sounds obvious, but the slope is slippery. If your "zone check" correlates with anxiety scores in your research, you *cannot* tell consumers that. If your attention training improves working memory in a clinical trial, you *cannot* claim it treats ADHD. The B2C product must live in the **wellness/lifestyle/skill-building** category, full stop.

The B2B product can get closer to clinical utility because:
- The buyer is an institution with professional oversight
- The "support dashboard" is for *supervisors*, not self-diagnosis
- Claims are about *operational readiness* and *support routing*, not treatment

But even B2B needs careful legal review if it touches NHS or insurance reimbursement.

## The Economic Climate Angle

In the UK/US 2026 context:

- **B2B is where the money is**—employer wellness budgets, NHS contracts, corporate L&D. But procurement is slow, evidence-hungry, and relationship-dependent.
- **B2C is where the speed is**—lower barrier to launch, faster iteration, direct user feedback. But unit economics are brutal, trust is low, and the "brain training" stigma means customer acquisition costs are high unless you have a genuinely differentiated pitch.
- **The "plug-in" framing for B2C** is your differentiation. You're not selling "cognitive enhancement"—you're selling **preparation for specific high-stakes contexts**. This is a much more defensible and marketable position.

Your instinct to keep B2C light, coach-mediated, and system-oriented is correct. The risk is in execution: the temptation to add "just a little" diagnostic value, "just a little" benchmarking, "just a little" clinical credibility. Each of those "just a littles" is a regulatory liability.

**Bottom line**: This two-tier architecture is strategically sound. The B2B engine generates revenue and evidence; the B2C layer generates reach, brand, and user volume. But they need firewalls—legal, product, and messaging—not just feature flags.
