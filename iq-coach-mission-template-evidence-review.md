# Evidence review: mission-template training for far transfer

## Purpose

This review updates the earlier evidence notes into a practical **mission-template** framework for IQ Coach / Trident-G training. The aim is to evaluate whether trainable cognitive strategies can support far transfer in applied domains such as comprehension, argumentation, decision-making, strategic action, learning, negotiation, collaboration and creative reframing.

The central claim is deliberately cautious:

> **The evidence supports a mission-template architecture more strongly than a generic brain-training claim.** Training is most defensible when it teaches compact reasoning templates, applies them across varied wrappers, links them to real-world missions, collects feedback and retests after delay.

This is not the same as claiming that a single reasoning drill or puzzle game raises psychometric *g*. The best-supported claim is that structured strategies can improve performance in relevant task families and may support transfer when the learner abstracts the underlying structure, practises it across contexts, deploys it under real cues and receives feedback.

---

## 1. Evidence grading used in this review

| Evidence grade | Meaning for IQ Coach |
|---|---|
| **Good** | Multiple studies, reviews or meta-analyses support training effects in the target domain or closely related transfer tasks. Still not automatically proof of broad far transfer. |
| **Moderate** | There is useful evidence for components or bounded transfer, but broader far transfer depends heavily on design, context and measurement. |
| **Weak-to-moderate** | The construct is plausible and useful, but direct evidence for transferable training is limited or domain-specific. |
| **Weak** | Evidence does not support broad claims, or effects are mostly near-transfer/task-specific. Use only as an evidence-generating hypothesis. |

The overall standard is conservative: strong component evidence does not by itself prove the full Trident-G far-transfer stack.

# 2. Core taxonomy of trainable cognitive strategy families

The following taxonomy treats strategies as **trainable templates** rather than theoretical operators. The goal is to convert abstract reasoning families into user-facing scripts that can be practised, varied and tested.

## 2.1 Deductive / constraint reasoning

**Trainable skill.** Derive what must follow, exclude impossibilities, identify contradictions, test counterexamples and preserve logical consistency.

**Example templates.**

```text
What must be true?
What cannot be true?
What follows if this claim is true?
What would contradict this?
What counterexample would break the rule?
```

**Training module.**

```text
Module: Constraint Check

1. State the claim, rule or goal.
2. List what must be true.
3. List what cannot be true.
4. Test one boundary case.
5. State the revised claim or next move.
```

**Evidence evaluation.** Evidence is **moderate**. Critical-thinking instruction has a positive but generally modest average effect. Abrami et al. (2015) found that critical-thinking instruction improves critical-thinking outcomes, with the strongest results when critical thinking is taught explicitly rather than merely assumed to emerge from subject teaching. This supports deductive/constraint templates as part of a broader argumentation, decision or problem-solving module, but not as proof of broad general intelligence transfer.

**Claim boundary.**

```text
Supported:
Constraint templates can train disciplined claim-testing and rule-checking.

Not yet supported:
A short deductive-reasoning module will broadly improve all real-world reasoning.
```

---

## 2.2 Inductive / relational abstraction

**Trainable skill.** Detect regularities, compare cases, extract common structure, form rules and identify boundary conditions.

**Example templates.**

```text
What is the same relation across these cases?
Which feature changed?
What rule explains all three examples?
Where does the rule stop working?
```

**Training module.**

```text
Module: Find the Invariant

1. Compare three cases.
2. Mark shared structure.
3. Mark surface differences.
4. Extract a candidate rule.
5. Test the rule on a new wrapper.
6. Record whether the same rule survived.
```

**Evidence evaluation.** Evidence is **good for structured transfer**. Klauer and Phye (2008) reviewed inductive-reasoning training and described it as comparison-based strategy training focused on regularities, irregularities and rule formation. This is one of the most relevant precedents for IQ Coach because it explicitly trains abstraction away from surface details. However, broad psychometric transfer remains a stronger claim than the evidence justifies.

**Claim boundary.**

```text
Supported:
Comparison-based training can improve inductive reasoning and support structured transfer.

Not yet supported:
Inductive training alone reliably raises broad intelligence.
```

---

## 2.3 Analogical reasoning

**Trainable skill.** Transfer structure by role rather than surface similarity. Identify what plays the same function in two different situations.

**Example templates.**

```text
What is the source case?
What is the target case?
What roles match?
What relation is preserved?
What implication transfers?
```

**Training module.**

```text
Module: Role Map

1. Select a solved source case.
2. Select a new target case.
3. Map roles, not labels.
4. State the shared relational structure.
5. Apply the source strategy to the target.
6. Check where the analogy breaks.
```

**Evidence evaluation.** Evidence is **good when comparison is explicit**. Gentner et al. (2003) showed that analogical encoding can help learners abstract a common schema and transfer it to later problems. This is especially important for negotiation and business-strategy missions, where the user must recognise that a superficially different case may share the same deep structure.

**Claim boundary.**

```text
Supported:
Analogical comparison can improve schema abstraction and transfer to related new cases.

Not yet supported:
Analogy training automatically transfers to all open-ended life or business problems.
```

---

## 2.4 Abductive / explanatory reasoning

**Trainable skill.** Generate rival explanations, infer the best explanation, identify causal structure and design tests that discriminate between models.

**Example templates.**

```text
What else could explain this?
What would each explanation predict?
Which explanation explains the most with the least strain?
What observation would separate them?
```

**Training module.**

```text
Module: Rival Models

1. State the surprising observation.
2. Generate at least two explanations.
3. List what each explanation predicts.
4. Identify a discriminating observation or test.
5. Update confidence after feedback.
```

**Evidence evaluation.** Evidence is **moderate but indirect**. The strongest support comes from self-explanation and scientific/critical-thinking instruction rather than from isolated abduction training. Bisra et al. (2018) found that self-explanation prompts improve learning, consistent with the idea that learners benefit when they generate causal or conceptual links. Abductive templates are therefore defensible inside comprehension, argumentation and decision modules, but less well supported as standalone far-transfer training.

**Claim boundary.**

```text
Supported:
Rival-explanation templates can support explanation, causal reasoning and model testing.

Not yet supported:
Standalone abduction training has robust broad far-transfer evidence.
```

---

## 2.5 Probabilistic / Bayesian reasoning

**Trainable skill.** Use base rates, likelihoods, uncertainty, calibration and evidence updating.

**Example templates.**

```text
Out of 100 similar cases, how many start in each category?
How often would this evidence appear if hypothesis A were true?
How often would it appear if hypothesis B were true?
How should confidence change?
```

**Training module.**

```text
Module: Base-Rate Update

1. Set the reference class.
2. Convert probabilities into natural frequencies.
3. Add the new evidence.
4. Compare evidence under each hypothesis.
5. Update confidence.
6. State the action implication.
```

**Evidence evaluation.** Evidence is **good for trained probabilistic formats and moderate for wider transfer**. McDowell and Jacobs (2017) meta-analysed the natural-frequency effect and found that Bayesian reasoning improves when information is represented as natural frequencies rather than conditional probabilities. This supports natural-frequency trees, 2×2 tables and “out of 100” templates. The wider transfer claim depends on whether learners can translate messy real-world information into these formats without being cued.

**Claim boundary.**

```text
Supported:
Natural-frequency templates improve Bayesian reasoning in relevant tasks.

Not yet supported:
One probabilistic-reasoning lesson produces broad decision competence across domains.
```

---

## 2.6 Value-of-information / probe selection

**Trainable skill.** Choose the next test that best separates competing possibilities, reduces uncertainty or changes the next action.

**Example templates.**

```text
What result would change my mind?
What is the cheapest useful test?
Which test best separates the options?
What information is not worth collecting?
```

**Training module.**

```text
Module: Next Best Probe

1. State the competing possibilities.
2. Identify what you still do not know.
3. Ask which unknown would change the decision.
4. Choose the cheapest useful test.
5. Run the test.
6. Update the map.
```

**Evidence evaluation.** Evidence is **weak-to-moderate** as a standalone training target. The theoretical fit is strong, especially for science, diagnosis, strategy, negotiation and entrepreneurship, but direct evidence for transferable value-of-information training is thinner than for self-explanation, inductive reasoning or implementation intentions. It is best embedded inside decision-making and strategic action missions.

**Claim boundary.**

```text
Supported:
Probe-selection templates are theoretically central to strategic action and uncertainty reduction.

Not yet supported:
Value-of-information training alone has strong broad far-transfer evidence.
```

---

## 2.7 Search / route reasoning

**Trainable skill.** Navigate from current state to target state through constraints, possible moves, subgoals and feedback.

**Example templates.**

```text
Where am I now?
Where am I trying to get to?
What moves are available?
What constraint blocks progress?
What is the next useful move?
```

**Training module.**

```text
Module: State–Goal–Route

1. Define current state.
2. Define target state.
3. List constraints.
4. List possible moves.
5. Choose the next move.
6. Act or simulate.
7. Read feedback.
8. Update the route.
```

**Evidence evaluation.** Evidence is **moderate-to-good in bounded domains**. Schema instruction improves problem-solving performance, especially in mathematics word problems. Peltier and Vannest (2017) found positive effects of schema instruction on elementary students’ problem-solving performance. This supports using bounded puzzle or business-mission problem spaces, but it does not prove that puzzle practice alone transfers to complex real-world planning.

**Claim boundary.**

```text
Supported:
Problem-space templates can improve structured problem solving in bounded domains.

Not yet supported:
Puzzle or route training automatically improves messy real-world strategy.
```

---

## 2.8 Structural-map reasoning

**Trainable skill.** Represent systems as nodes, links, dependencies, bottlenecks, paths, feedback loops and constraints.

**Example templates.**

```text
What are the key nodes?
What depends on what?
Where is the bottleneck?
Which path is still open?
What feedback loop is operating?
```

**Training module.**

```text
Module: Structure Map

1. Draw the nodes.
2. Draw the dependencies.
3. Mark bottlenecks.
4. Mark feedback loops.
5. Identify one leverage point.
6. Test whether acting there changes the system.
```

**Evidence evaluation.** Evidence is **promising but less direct as a general far-transfer skill**. Concept mapping has meta-analytic support for learning and conceptual understanding (Nesbit & Adesope, 2006; Schroeder et al., 2018). However, the leap from concept mapping to general strategic systems reasoning needs direct validation. Structural mapping is best used as a tool inside comprehension, planning, collaboration and negotiation missions.

**Claim boundary.**

```text
Supported:
Concept and structure maps can support learning and understanding.

Not yet supported:
Generic graph-mapping training reliably transfers to all complex systems.
```

# 3. Mission-level taxonomy and training modules

The strongest product architecture is not “teach reasoning styles in isolation”. It is:

```text
reasoning strategy
→ applied mission frame
→ compact template
→ varied wrapper
→ real-world mission
→ feedback signal
→ delayed re-check
```

This makes far transfer an empirical question rather than a marketing assumption. A template counts as a candidate far-transfer mindware script only if it survives changed wrappers, delayed reuse and real-world deployment.

---

## 3.1 Module 1: Comprehend / Explain

**Core question.**

```text
What is happening, and why?
```

**Primary template.**

```text
Situation → variables → relations → explanation → prediction → boundary
```

**User-facing scripts.**

```text
What are the key variables?
What depends on what?
What explains the pattern?
What would this explanation predict next?
Where might this explanation fail?
```

**Training sequence.**

```text
1. Explain a simple visual or verbal case.
2. Compare two cases with the same structure.
3. Build a short causal or concept map.
4. Predict what should happen next.
5. Test the prediction in a changed wrapper.
6. Explain the relation in one sentence.
```

**Evidence evaluation.** Evidence is **good for learning and comprehension outcomes**. Self-explanation prompts improve learning by encouraging learners to generate causal and conceptual links (Bisra et al., 2018). Concept maps also support learning across domains (Nesbit & Adesope, 2006; Schroeder et al., 2018). Hattie and Donoghue (2016) argue that learning strategies are phase-sensitive, with different strategies supporting surface, deep and transfer phases.

**Transfer claim.**

```text
Strong:
Comprehension templates can improve explanation quality and structured understanding.

Cautious:
Transfer to unrelated real-world comprehension tasks must be measured through wrapper swaps and delayed probes.
```

**Example real-world mission.**

```text
Choose one confusing situation today.
Write:
situation → variables → relation → explanation → prediction.
Check later whether the prediction helped.
```

---

## 3.2 Module 2: Argue / Evaluate Claims

**Core question.**

```text
What is the claim, what supports it, and where does it stop being true?
```

**Primary template.**

```text
claim → evidence → warrant → objection → boundary → revised claim
```

**User-facing scripts.**

```text
What exactly is being claimed?
What evidence supports it?
What assumption links the evidence to the claim?
What would make it wrong?
What is the strongest fair objection?
What is the narrower, safer claim?
```

**Training sequence.**

```text
1. Identify the claim.
2. Separate evidence from interpretation.
3. Add the warrant or assumption.
4. Generate one counterexample.
5. Revise the claim boundary.
6. Compare the revised claim across a new topic.
```

**Evidence evaluation.** Evidence is **moderate**. Critical-thinking instruction produces positive but modest effects, especially when argument analysis, dialogue, problem solving or explicit critical-thinking instruction are used (Abrami et al., 2015). The transfer target should therefore be claim-support discipline, not a broad claim that argument training automatically improves all reasoning.

**Transfer claim.**

```text
Strong:
Argument templates can improve explicit claim evaluation and counter-consideration.

Cautious:
Broad everyday argumentation transfer requires varied topics, delayed checks and real-world use.
```

**Example real-world mission.**

```text
Before accepting a claim online or at work, write:
claim → evidence → assumption → counterexample → safer claim.
```

---

## 3.3 Module 3: Decide / Choose

**Core question.**

```text
Which option should I choose under uncertainty and trade-offs?
```

**Primary template.**

```text
options → criteria → probabilities/base rates → trade-offs → choice → uncertainty note
```

**User-facing scripts.**

```text
What are the real options?
What criteria matter most?
What are the base rates?
What could go wrong?
What would change the choice?
What is the least-regret next step?
```

**Training sequence.**

```text
1. Compare two or three options.
2. Weight criteria.
3. Add base-rate or natural-frequency information.
4. Run a premortem.
5. Choose.
6. State uncertainty.
7. Identify one feedback signal.
```

**Evidence evaluation.** Evidence is **moderate**. Natural-frequency formats improve Bayesian reasoning (McDowell & Jacobs, 2017). Debiasing training has promising evidence, including field transfer for confirmation-bias reduction in professional decision contexts (Sellier et al., 2019), and earlier work shows that a single training intervention can reduce several decision biases in laboratory and transfer tasks (Morewedge et al., 2015). However, broad “better decision-making” is difficult to prove unless outcomes are clearly measured.

**Transfer claim.**

```text
Strong:
Decision templates can improve structured analysis of evidence, uncertainty and trade-offs.

Cautious:
Real-world decision quality depends on feedback quality, stakes, incentives and context.
```

**Example real-world mission.**

```text
Use the template for one real decision today.
Record:
choice → uncertainty → feedback signal → what I learned.
```

## 3.4 Module 4: Plan & Act / Strategic Problem-Space Control

**Core question.**

```text
What is the state, goal, constraint, next action and feedback signal?
```

**Primary template.**

```text
current state → target state → constraints → available moves → next test → action → feedback → update
```

**User-facing scripts.**

```text
Where am I now?
Where am I trying to get?
What is blocking progress?
What moves are available?
Which next action gives the most useful feedback?
What signal will tell me whether it worked?
```

**Why this should not be called generic strategic planning.**

For a CEO, founder or entrepreneur, “planning” bundles goal selection, subgoal decomposition, prioritisation, resource allocation, stakeholder modelling, uncertainty management, sequencing, delegation, feedback interpretation and pivot/persist decisions. This is too broad for a clean training claim. The better construct is **strategic problem-space control**:

```text
strategic planning = problem-space construction + route control + feedback updating
```

Complex problem-solving research is consistent with this interpretation: complex problems involve a start state, a target state and barriers that may be dynamic, partially intransparent and initially uncertain (Dörner & Funke, 2017). Business-planning evidence is also contextual: Brinckmann et al. (2010) found that planning tends to benefit small firms, but its relationship with performance is moderated by context, including firm newness and environment.

**Training sequence.**

```text
1. Build the state map.
2. Define target state and subgoals.
3. Identify constraints and bottlenecks.
4. Choose the next best test.
5. Act.
6. Record the feedback signal.
7. Update: continue, revise, delegate, stop or pivot.
```

**Evidence evaluation.** Evidence is **strong for action deployment, moderate for component problem-space strategies, and weak-to-moderate for broad strategic planning training**. Goal-setting theory is well supported, particularly for specific and difficult goals under appropriate conditions (Locke & Latham, 2002). Implementation intentions strongly support cue-linked action deployment (Gollwitzer & Sheeran, 2006). MCII/WOOP-style obstacle planning has meta-analytic support for goal attainment (Wang et al., 2021). Transfer-of-training evidence also shows that real transfer depends on trainee characteristics, training design and the work environment (Blume et al., 2010). However, scenario-planning reviews note conceptual and methodological confusion and limited evidence on effectiveness (Cordova-Pozo & Rouwette, 2023).

**Transfer claim.**

```text
Strong:
Goal setting, if–then planning, obstacle planning and feedback loops support action deployment.

Cautious:
Broad CEO/entrepreneur strategic-planning transfer is not established.
The defensible training target is strategic problem-space control.
```

**Example founder/CEO mission.**

```text
Choose one current business problem.
Write:
state → goal → bottleneck → next test → feedback signal.
Run the test within 24 hours.
Record whether the map changed.
```

**Submodules.**

```text
Priority Map
What matters most now, and why?

Bottleneck Map
Which constraint is currently limiting progress?

Subgoal Ladder
What intermediate state must be reached before the main goal is possible?

Next-Test Plan
What small action will reduce the most uncertainty?

Pivot / Persist Review
What feedback would justify continuing, changing, or stopping?

Delegation Map
Which action requires me, and which can move without me?

Feedback Loop
What signal will tell me whether the action worked?
```

## 3.5 Module 5: Learn / Acquire

**Core question.**

```text
How should I acquire, practise, test and transfer this?
```

**Primary template.**

```text
goal skill → worked example → retrieval → feedback → variation → delayed test → transfer case
```

**User-facing scripts.**

```text
What am I trying to learn?
What is a worked example?
Can I retrieve it without looking?
What feedback did I get?
Can I use it in a new wrapper?
Can I still use it tomorrow?
```

**Training sequence.**

```text
1. Choose a learning target.
2. Study a worked example.
3. Self-explain the example.
4. Retrieve without looking.
5. Practise with variation.
6. Test after delay.
7. Apply in a new wrapper.
```

**Evidence evaluation.** Evidence is **good for learning outcomes, but not evidence of g increase**. Learning-strategy instruction improves academic performance (Donker et al., 2014). Self-regulated learning interventions improve learning outcomes, strategy use and motivation (Dignath et al., 2008). Hattie and Donoghue (2016) emphasise that strategy effectiveness depends on learning phase, which supports an IQ Coach design that distinguishes surface acquisition, deeper understanding and transfer testing.

**Transfer claim.**

```text
Strong:
Learning templates can improve acquisition, monitoring, retrieval and transfer preparation.

Cautious:
This is learning-strategy transfer, not direct intelligence enhancement.
```

**Example real-world mission.**

```text
Pick one skill or concept.
Use:
worked example → self-explain → retrieve → varied practice → delayed re-check.
```

## 3.6 Module 6: Negotiate / Coordinate

**Core question.**

```text
What do they want, what do I want, and what trade can improve both sides?
```

**Primary template.**

```text
my interests → their interests → BATNA → ZOPA → tradable issues → first probe
```

**User-facing scripts.**

```text
What do I really need?
What do they really need?
What is my alternative if no agreement happens?
What range could work for both sides?
Which issue can be traded rather than fought over?
What first question reveals useful information?
```

**Training sequence.**

```text
1. Separate positions from interests.
2. Identify each side’s alternative.
3. Estimate the possible agreement zone.
4. List tradable issues.
5. Choose a low-risk information probe.
6. Practise a fair offer.
7. Review the outcome.
```

**Evidence evaluation.** Evidence is **moderate and worth including**. Negotiation training appears to improve performance, with ElShenawy (2010) reporting that training level was associated with better individual and joint performance. Analogical encoding is especially relevant: Gentner et al. (2003) found that comparing negotiation cases supported schema abstraction and transfer. Negotiation is therefore an excellent mission category for IQ Coach because it combines analogy, decision-making, perspective-taking, argumentation, value trade-offs and strategic action.

**Transfer claim.**

```text
Strong:
Negotiation templates can improve structured preparation and case-to-case transfer.

Cautious:
Durable real-world negotiation transfer needs longer practice, feedback and varied cases.
```

**Example real-world mission.**

```text
Before one real conversation, write:
my interests → their interests → BATNA → tradable issue → first probe.
Afterwards, record what you learned.
```

## 3.7 Module 7: Collaborate / Solve Together

**Core question.**

```text
How do we build a shared map and act together?
```

**Primary template.**

```text
shared goal → roles → information gaps → disagreement → joint next test → review
```

**User-facing scripts.**

```text
What are we trying to achieve together?
Who owns which part?
What does each person know?
Where do we disagree?
What test would move the group forward?
When do we review?
```

**Training sequence.**

```text
1. Build a shared situation map.
2. Identify roles and constraints.
3. Surface one disagreement.
4. Convert disagreement into a test.
5. Assign next actions.
6. Review feedback.
```

**Evidence evaluation.** Evidence is **moderate-to-weak as a general far-transfer category**. Collaboration overlaps with negotiation, learning, feedback, communication and team problem solving. The evidence is stronger when collaboration is trained through specific behaviours, scripts, roles and feedback than when it is treated as a vague “teamwork skill”. For IQ Coach, collaboration should be treated as a later mission family requiring its own outcome measures.

**Transfer claim.**

```text
Supported:
Shared-map and review templates can structure collaborative problem solving.

Not yet supported:
Generic collaboration training reliably transfers across all teams and contexts.
```

**Example real-world mission.**

```text
In one team discussion, use:
shared goal → roles → disagreement → next test → review point.
```

## 3.8 Module 8: Create / Reframe

**Core question.**

```text
How else could this be represented or solved?
```

**Primary template.**

```text
current frame → constraint → alternative frame → analogy → prototype → test
```

**User-facing scripts.**

```text
What assumption is locking the frame?
What constraint could be removed or inverted?
What analogy suggests another route?
What small prototype could test the new frame?
```

**Training sequence.**

```text
1. State the current frame.
2. Identify the blocking assumption.
3. Generate two alternative representations.
4. Use one analogy.
5. Build a tiny prototype or test.
6. Compare outcomes.
```

**Evidence evaluation.** Evidence is **mixed**. Training can improve task-specific creativity, ideation and problem-representation skills, especially when methods are practised directly. However, broad far transfer in creative problem-solving is difficult to demonstrate. The strongest Trident-G interpretation is to treat creative reframing as a mission that must be validated through changed wrappers, delayed use and real-world prototypes.

**Transfer claim.**

```text
Supported:
Reframing templates can improve local representational flexibility and ideation.

Not yet supported:
Short creativity training reliably produces broad creative far transfer.
```

**Example real-world mission.**

```text
Choose one stuck problem.
Write:
current frame → hidden assumption → opposite frame → small prototype.
Run the prototype or define the feedback signal.
```

## 3.9 Module 9: Appraisal / Self-Regulation

**Core question.**

```text
What am I predicting, and what small test could update it?
```

**Primary template.**

```text
trigger → body signal → prediction → meaning → action urge → small probe → outcome
```

**User-facing scripts.**

```text
What triggered the reaction?
What did my body signal?
What was I predicting?
What meaning did I attach?
What action urge appeared?
What small safe probe could test the prediction?
```

**Training sequence.**

```text
1. Identify the trigger.
2. Separate body signal, prediction and meaning.
3. Generate an alternative model.
4. Choose a small probe.
5. Record outcome.
6. Recheck later.
```

**Evidence evaluation.** Evidence is **moderate for component mechanisms**, but this belongs primarily to Resilience Coach rather than IQ Coach. It is consistent with cognitive reappraisal, self-regulation and implementation-intention literatures, but should not be framed as a clinical intervention without separate validation.

**Transfer claim.**

```text
Supported:
Appraisal templates can structure adaptive self-regulation practice.

Not yet supported:
Clinical treatment claims or broad resilience-transfer claims without controlled validation.
```

# 4. Where evidence is strongest

The strongest evidence is for **component mechanisms**, not the full far-transfer stack.

| Component | Evidence strength | Product implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Self-explanation** | Good | Use in comprehension, learning, argumentation and puzzle debriefs. |
| **Inductive / analogical comparison** | Good | Use across all modules to extract invariants across cases. |
| **Natural-frequency Bayesian reasoning** | Good for trained formats | Use in decision modules, but vary syntax and context. |
| **Learning strategy instruction / SRL** | Good for learning outcomes | Use in Learn/Acquire modules and training plans. |
| **Implementation intentions** | Good for deployment | Use in Plan & Act and real-world missions. |
| **Goal setting** | Good for performance under appropriate conditions | Use for subgoal clarity, not as the whole strategy. |
| **Schema/problem-space instruction** | Moderate-to-good in bounded domains | Use in puzzles and structured business/problem missions. |
| **Training-transfer design** | Moderate | Design for near, intermediate and far transfer, with environment support. |

---

# 5. Where evidence is weaker

| Claim | Evidence status | Safe response |
|---|---|---|
| “Generic reasoning training broadly raises *g*.” | Weak | Do not claim this. Measure untrained reasoning outcomes instead. |
| “Puzzles alone improve decision-making, planning or argumentation.” | Weak | Pair puzzles with prompts, varied wrappers, missions and delayed probes. |
| “Strategic planning training transfers broadly to CEO performance.” | Weak-to-moderate | Reframe as strategic problem-space control and test in live missions. |
| “Scenario planning reliably improves strategic outcomes.” | Weak-to-moderate | Use as one tool for uncertainty exploration, not as a guaranteed intervention. |
| “Debiasing training produces broad decision competence.” | Mixed | Use bias modules as part of repeated decision practice and feedback. |
| “Value-of-information reasoning transfers as a standalone skill.” | Weak-to-moderate | Embed inside decision, argumentation, negotiation and strategic-action modules. |
| “Negotiation training reliably produces long-term real-world transfer.” | Moderate but incomplete | Use varied cases, feedback and delayed missions. |
| “Creativity training reliably produces broad far transfer.” | Mixed | Treat as reframing/prototyping practice, not guaranteed creativity enhancement. |

# 6. Recommended IQ Coach training architecture

## 6.1 Core structure for every module

Each training module should follow the same transfer logic:

```text
1. Introduce one compact template.
2. Demonstrate it on a simple case.
3. Practise it on an in-app problem.
4. Swap wrapper while preserving the same template.
5. Use a short reflection prompt.
6. Assign a real-world mission.
7. Record a feedback signal.
8. Re-test after delay.
```

## 6.2 Why this is better than generic “strategy training”

Generic strategy training often fails because it does not specify:

```text
what structure is being trained
what cue should activate it
what wrapper changes test portability
what feedback signal tells the learner it worked
what delayed probe tests retention
```

The mission-template model specifies these elements explicitly. It turns each strategy into a testable unit:

```text
template → wrapper variation → action cue → feedback → delayed reuse
```

## 6.3 Core validation metrics

| Transfer layer | Metric examples |
|---|---|
| **In-app learning** | accuracy, completion time, error type, explanation quality |
| **Wrapper transfer** | performance after changed domain, syntax, surface or case |
| **Delayed retention** | next-day or next-week template reuse |
| **Niche transfer** | real-world mission completion, cue detection, feedback recording |
| **Quality of action** | clearer next action, useful feedback signal, better update |
| **Untrained reasoning** | short reasoning transfer tests, decision quality vignettes, argument evaluation tasks |
| **Self-report** | perceived clarity, confidence calibration, cognitive effort, usefulness |

# 7. Updated claim boundary

## 7.1 Safe claims

```text
IQ Coach trains applied reasoning templates for comprehension, argumentation,
decision-making, strategic action, learning and negotiation.

It tests whether these templates survive changed wrappers, delayed probes and
real-world missions.

The strongest evidence supports component mechanisms such as self-explanation,
inductive and analogical comparison, learning strategies, implementation
intentions, natural-frequency reasoning and bounded problem-space instruction.
```

## 7.2 Avoid or soften

```text
IQ Coach raises IQ.
IQ Coach raises g.
IQ Coach guarantees far transfer.
Puzzle training automatically improves real-world reasoning.
Strategic planning training makes users better CEOs or entrepreneurs.
Decision-bias training produces general rationality.
```

## 7.3 Best overall formulation

> **IQ Coach is an evidence-generating applied reasoning system. It trains compact mission templates, varies the surface wrapper, links templates to real-world cues and feedback, and tests whether users can reuse the same structure after delay and across contexts.**
