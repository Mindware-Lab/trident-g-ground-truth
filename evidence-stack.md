# Evidence review: CCC, working memory, relational processing and transfer architecture in the Trident-G protocol

## Overview

The Trident-G protocol is built around a layered transfer claim. Lower-level state and attention-control training should support working-memory binding and relational processing, which should then be connected to language-accessible prompts, problem-space tasks, implementation intentions, delayed probes and real-world deployment. In the source protocol, far transfer is explicitly defined as more than repeated task improvement: it requires Ψ-band access, predictive relational-map tuning, wrapper variation, vertical control-policy integration, niche coupling and consolidation into slow schematic Gc. 

This review separates the evidence into eight layers:

1. **CCC / MFT-M training**: evidence for controlled evidence-throughput measurement and selective transfer.
2. **Working-memory training**: evidence for near transfer to WM, but weak evidence for broad far transfer.
3. **Relational working memory / binding**: evidence that WMC is partly about maintaining temporary bindings and relations.
4. **Relational processing and Gf**: evidence that relation processing is central to fluid intelligence.
5. **Successor representations and predictive maps**: evidence for state → transition → future-state representations.
6. **Vertical transfer**: evidence for combining lower-level skills with strategy, metacognition and implementation layers.
7. **Horizontal transfer**: the less directly established but theoretically important principle of wrapper swaps, breakpoint perturbation and invariant recovery.
8. **Offline consolidation**: evidence that sleep, spacing and delayed re-checks support durable learning, abstraction and some forms of insight.

The key claim boundary is this: **the components have meaningful evidence, but the complete Trident-G CCC → relational WM → puzzle/meta-prompt → implementation → delayed transfer stack still requires direct validation.** This fits the existing Trident-G claim boundary: the architecture is evidence-generating rather than already proven as a strong psychometric g-enhancement intervention.

## 1. CCC / MFT-M training: controlled evidence throughput and selective transfer

The Majority Function Task with masking (MFT-M) provides the most direct evidence base for the **Zone / cognitive bandwidth** layer of Trident-G. Wu et al. (2016) modelled cognitive-control capacity as a limited information channel, estimating how much task-relevant perceptual information participants could bring under control per second. The task manipulates majority ratio and exposure time, then fits accuracy across conditions to estimate CCC in bits per second. The attached evidence notes summarise this as a model-based estimate of task-relevant information controlled under perceptual uncertainty, with typical estimates around 3–4 bps. 

He et al. (2022) then adapted this into a more efficient computerised adaptive test. Instead of administering all MFT-M conditions, the adaptive version selects the next condition that is most informative for the current CCC estimate. This supports the Trident-G design principle that training should be routed around a **trainable threshold zone**, rather than simply making a task harder after correct responses.

The most relevant training evidence comes from Zhang et al. (2024), who trained healthy young adults on MFT-M for seven days and found increased CCC plus selective transfer to attention-control, verbal-learning and ERP indices of working-memory/task-switching processing. Importantly, the transfer was patterned rather than global: behavioural transfer was strongest where the transfer task shared attentional-selection structure with MFT-M, and neural transfer appeared more clearly than behavioural gains on some executive tasks (Zhang et al., 2024).  

This fits the Trident-G interpretation: MFT-M plausibly trains **controlled evidence extraction**, attentional allocation and uncertainty reduction. It does not, by itself, train arbitrary binding, relational transformation tracking, SR-style horizon mapping or explicit problem-space control. The attached evidence document makes the same point: MFT-M appears to support the lower half of the stack, but not the full far-transfer architecture. 

**Trident-G interpretation:** CCC/MFT-M is best treated as a **state and evidence-control layer**, not a complete intelligence-training protocol. It provides a behavioural readiness and bandwidth estimate that can gate whether the learner should proceed, stabilise, reset or reduce difficulty.

## 2. Working-memory training: useful near transfer, weak far transfer by itself

The working-memory training literature gives a useful but cautionary foundation. Melby-Lervåg et al. (2016) concluded that WM training produces reliable improvement on trained and closely related WM tasks, but no convincing evidence for broad far transfer to intelligence or other general cognitive outcomes when compared with active controls.  

This is not a reason to exclude WM training. It is a reason to avoid the claim:

> WM training alone raises intelligence.

A more defensible claim is:

> WM training can improve performance on trained and structurally similar WM tasks, and may provide a useful substrate when embedded in a broader transfer architecture.

Gathercole et al. (2019) sharpen this interpretation by arguing that WM training may often involve learning new task-specific cognitive routines, with transfer depending on the overlap between trained and untrained task structures. Their account fits the Trident-G distinction between **surface skill / fast Gc** and deeper schematic transfer: the learner may become highly efficient at a trained WM routine without acquiring a portable relational control policy.

Ericson and Klingberg’s (2023) dual-process model also helps here. They distinguish a **form route**, where training produces task- or format-specific gains, from a **function route**, where training modifies a more general cognitive function. This maps well onto the Trident-G distinction between surface-bound fast Gc and deeper function-level change.

**Trident-G interpretation:** ordinary WM training is useful but insufficient. It should be embedded inside a protocol that trains variable abstraction, binding, relational transformation, wrapper recovery, global-workspace prompting and delayed deployment.

## 3. Working memory as relational binding: the basis for R-WMC

The rationale for **Relational Working Memory Capacity** is grounded in a serious tradition that treats WMC as more than item storage.

Oberauer’s binding account is central. He argues that WM capacity limits the short-term maintenance of **temporary bindings**, such as which item was linked to which other item, position or context. In his binding-memory experiments, increasing set size impaired binding memory more than item memory, supporting the idea that WMC is partly a limit on temporary relational structure (Oberauer, 2019).  

This supports the Trident-G move from classic item n-back:

```text
item identity
→ hold over n steps
→ compare current item with previous item
```

to relational n-back:

```text
feature field
→ extract majority state
→ bind features or relations
→ maintain across n steps
→ compare arbitrary associations or transformations
```

The proposed Gabor/optic-flow task therefore extends WM measurement from item updating into:

```text
feature discrimination
→ arbitrary binding
→ relational transformation
→ successor/path prediction
```

The role of WM in perceptual transfer is also relevant. Zhang et al. (2016) argued that transfer in auditory discrimination learning can be enhanced by across-trial variation and that working memory may mediate this enhanced transfer. This supports the idea that discrimination learning and WM are not separate silos: variable abstraction and WM binding may interact when training needs to generalise across surface variation.

**Trident-G interpretation:** R-WMC is a stronger candidate than simple n-back level because it measures how much structured relational information can be extracted, bound, updated and protected over short temporal horizons.

## 4. Relational processing, relational integration training and fluid intelligence

Relational processing provides the main theoretical bridge from WM to Gf. Chuderski’s (2014) relational integration task was designed to measure the ability to bind mental representations into more complex relational structures, and it predicted fluid reasoning above other WM tasks.

Jastrzębski et al. (2020) extend this further. Their work suggests that a factor capturing relation-processing variance can be statistically equivalent to a Gf factor loaded by hallmark fluid-reasoning tests, with much shared residual variance unexplained by WMC alone. This is important because it implies that the bridge from WMC to Gf may specifically run through **relation processing**, not just generic storage capacity.  

Wang et al. (2025) provide especially relevant mechanistic evidence for the relational-processing layer of the Trident-G protocol. The study used a randomised controlled design in which participants were assigned to either a relational integration training group or an active control group for one month. Fluid intelligence was assessed with the Sandia Matrices task, and resting EEG was recorded before and after training. The authors frame relational integration as a key subcomponent of working memory and a strong predictor of fluid intelligence, with both relational integration and Gf relying substantially on frontoparietal network function (Wang et al., 2025).  

The training task was more relationally demanding than standard item n-back. Participants had to judge whether a numerical relation or difference matched a previous relation, with both 1-back and 2-back versions. In other words, the trained unit was not simply item identity but the **relation between items across temporal positions**. This is directly aligned with the Trident-G distinction between item-memory training and relational workspace training.

The strongest findings were neural. After training, the relational integration group showed changes in EEG microstate dynamics associated with the frontoparietal network. The authors reported increased microstate D occurrence and contribution, with microstate D occurrence negatively related to reaction time, suggesting that greater expression of this state was associated with faster relational reasoning performance. The training group also showed reduced microstate C occurrence and contribution relative to controls, plus altered transition probabilities. Wang et al. interpret these findings as evidence that relational integration training influenced frontoparietal networks associated with fluid intelligence.

For Trident-G, the important point is that this was not merely generic WM training. It trained a relation-preserving operation:

```text
current relation
→ relation n steps back
→ compare structural match
→ respond
```

That makes it a better fit for fluid intelligence than standard item n-back, because Gf tasks such as matrix reasoning require the learner to hold and compare transformations, dimensions, constraints and relations, not just repeat item identities.

The correct interpretation is not:

> Relational n-back has been proven to raise IQ.

It is:

> Relational integration training targets a more Gf-relevant operation than item n-back because the trained unit is relational structure rather than item identity.

This supports the design principle:

```text
train relations, not items
scale relational complexity before simply raising n
test whether the relation survives surface changes
```

**Trident-G interpretation:** relational processing is the best-supported cognitive target for connecting WM training to Gf-style reasoning. Wang et al. (2025) strengthen the case that relational integration training engages a plausible Gf-relevant neural system, while leaving open the stronger claim that wrapper-swapped, vertically integrated relational training produces robust far transfer.

## 5. Successor representations and predictive relational maps

The SR layer gives the Trident-G protocol a computational target beyond simple relation matching. Stachenfeld et al. (2017) argue that the hippocampus can be understood as a predictive map, representing each state partly in terms of the states likely to follow from it.

This matters because far transfer requires more than recognising a repeated relation. It often requires knowing:

```text
current state
→ possible transition
→ likely consequence
→ next reachable state
```

Gershman’s (2018) broader account of successor representations similarly defines SRs as encoding environmental states in terms of their predictive relationships with other states. Human evidence from visual sequence learning also suggests that visual and hippocampal systems can develop successor-like predictive representations of future events (Ekman et al., 2023).

**Trident-G interpretation:** SR-inspired tasks should not be claimed to directly measure hippocampal SRs. The safer claim is that they train **behavioural successor-like inference**: tracking whether a current state can lead to a later state under a learned relation.

## 6. Vertical transfer: strongest evidence when lower-level skills are combined with strategy/metacognitive layers

The evidence for the exact Trident-G vertical-transfer stack is not direct yet. However, the broader literature supports the principle that lower-level cognitive training is more promising when combined with higher-level strategies, prompts and deployment mechanisms.

The source protocol defines vertical transfer as the reconstruction of a control policy across cognitive layers:

```text
Ψ-band access
→ controlled attention
→ WM variable binding
→ SR-style transition inference
→ global-workspace control policy
→ action selection
→ real-world feedback
→ delayed reuse
```

Vertical transfer is therefore not merely knowing a rule. A vertically integrated policy can be entered from the right state, held in WM, used to guide SR search, broadcast as a control policy, deployed under cue conditions, and later recovered after delay. 

Several empirical strands support this kind of layered view. First, strategy-based WM training provides a more promising route than pure drill. Chan et al. (2019) tested strategy-based WM training in children and reported transfer to a novel problem-solving task, suggesting that explicit strategy training can produce transfer beyond the trained WM surface.

Second, metacognitive or strategy-enriched WM interventions can produce maintained WM benefits. Jones et al. (2020) compared Cogmed WM training, Cogmed plus metacognitive strategy training, and adaptive visual-search control training in children. The combined WM and metacognitive strategy condition produced stronger maintained WM benefits than WM training alone, supporting the idea that metacognitive strategy instruction can extend the value of lower-level training (Jones et al., 2020).  

Third, the previously reviewed metacognitive evidence supports the higher layers: self-explanation, inductive-reasoning training, metacognitive strategy instruction, strategic problem-space control and implementation intentions. The important Trident-G point is that these should not be treated as separate interventions. They are vertical connectors:

```text
attention / WM capacity
→ relation tracking
→ puzzle-state representation
→ meta-epistemic prompt
→ implementation cue
→ delayed reuse
```

**Trident-G interpretation:** vertical transfer has better supporting evidence than horizontal wrapper-swap transfer, because many component literatures already show benefits of combining skill practice with explicit strategies, metacognition or implementation planning. The full vertical chain still needs direct experimental validation.

## 7. Horizontal transfer, wrapper swaps and breakpoint-driven learning: promising but less directly established

The horizontal-transfer principle is theoretically strong but empirically less directly established. In Trident-G, horizontal transfer means that the same relational invariant survives changed wrappers, boundary cases, novel contexts and delayed probes. The relevant test is not simply whether performance improves on the trained task, but whether the same structure can be recovered when the surface changes. 

The strongest adjacent support comes from several literatures.

First, the perceptual-learning literature shows that transfer is often limited by surface specificity, but can sometimes be improved through training design. Yu (2011) reviews the specificity/transfer problem in visual perceptual learning, noting that visual perceptual learning is often location- and orientation-specific but that later work challenges a purely low-level view. Zhang et al. (2010) proposed a rule-based learning account in which a high-level decision unit learns rules for reweighting perceptual inputs, with transfer depending partly on whether the relevant inputs are functionally connected to the decision rule.

Second, training time course and variation appear to matter. Larcombe et al. (2017) found that spreading training across five days produced transfer across visual hemifields and tasks, whereas condensing the same number of trials into one day did not. This supports the idea that consolidation and time structure affect whether learning remains surface-bound or becomes more portable.

Third, Donner and Hardy’s (2015) piecewise power-law account supports the idea that individual learning curves may contain breakpoints or transitions rather than one smooth function. The piecewise model better explained individual learning curves, and later pieces often surpassed earlier pieces after a brief transition cost. This does not prove the wrapper-swap principle, but it supports the plausibility of designing training around controlled perturbations, dip-and-recovery patterns and local reorganisation.

Fourth, Zhang and Tang’s (2025) PNAS paper provides a formal analogy for the Trident-G entropy–mutual-information balance. They identify critical-like signatures during neural-network training and argue that heavy-tailed update distributions arise from information-driven self-organisation, balancing maximum entropy exploration with mutual-information constraint. This is not human cognitive-training evidence, but it gives a useful formal model for why productive learning should not collapse into rigid exploitation or unstructured exploration.  

So the evidence status is:

| Claim                                                                          | Evidence status                                       |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ----------------------------------------------------- |
| Perceptual and cognitive training are often surface-specific                   | Strong                                                |
| Transfer can improve under some variation, time-course and exposure conditions | Moderate                                              |
| Learning curves may show breakpoints and transition costs                      | Moderate                                              |
| Wrapper swaps preserve invariants and force slow schematic Gc                  | Theoretically plausible, but not directly established |
| Entropy–MI balance explains human cognitive far transfer                       | Formal/theoretical support only so far                |

**Trident-G interpretation:** horizontal transfer should be treated as a design and measurement principle, not a proven mechanism. The app should measure wrapper-swap cost, recovery slope, delayed survival and cross-wrapper performance rather than assume transfer.

## 8. Offline consolidation, spacing and sleep: from task improvement to schematic generalisation

A further component of the Trident-G protocol is **offline consolidation**. This includes sleep, training breaks, distributed practice, delayed re-checks and recovery-sensitive scheduling. In Trident-G terms, the purpose is not merely to prevent fatigue. It is to allow trained relations, strategies and problem-space structures to stabilise, reorganise and become available for later re-entry under new task demands.

This matters because the Trident-G protocol distinguishes **flat task improvement** from **spiral advance**. Flat improvement means the learner becomes better at the immediate training surface. Spiral advance means the learner returns later with a richer representational starting point because some deeper relational invariant has been extracted. The source theory explicitly states that without consolidation, schematic geometry is not installed and the loop degrades into flat cycling rather than self-enriching spiralling. 

### Sleep and systems consolidation

The sleep literature provides a strong basis for treating consolidation as part of the protocol. Active systems consolidation theory proposes that sleep supports the redistribution and reorganisation of newly encoded memories, especially through hippocampal–neocortical interaction during slow-wave sleep (Born & Wilhelm, 2012). More recent reviews emphasise replay as a basic mechanism of memory consolidation during both sleep and wake, with sleep replay involving hippocampal assemblies, ripples, thalamic spindles and neocortical slow oscillations (Brodt et al., 2023). 

This maps well onto the Trident-G view that relational/SR training should not simply be pushed harder within a single session. If the target is slow schematic Gc, the system needs intervals in which recent patterns can be replayed, integrated and partially decoupled from the surface conditions in which they were learned.

### Sleep, gist abstraction and hidden regularities

Sleep is also relevant to **gist extraction** and hidden-structure discovery. Lerner and Gluck (2019) reviewed evidence that sleep can support extraction of hidden regularities, especially when tasks contain associative structures or embedded regularities not explicitly stated to the learner. This is close to the Trident-G claim that repeated exposure to relational structure across varied wrappers should allow slow abstraction of the invariant.

The classic Number Reduction Task study by Wagner et al. (2004) is especially relevant to problem/strategy insight. Participants initially learned a task containing a hidden abstract rule; after sleep, more than twice as many participants discovered the hidden rule compared with wake controls. The authors concluded that sleep can restructure memory representations and facilitate explicit insight.

However, this should be framed carefully. Later studies show that sleep does not reliably improve all forms of insight problem solving. Schönauer et al. (2018), for example, found no sleep benefit for classical insight problems and magic-trick problem-solving tasks. So the defensible claim is not “sleep guarantees insight”, but rather that sleep can support consolidation, restructuring and abstraction under some learning conditions, especially where the problem has been partially encoded beforehand.

### Spacing, distributed practice and durable learning

Offline consolidation is not only about sleep. Distributed practice is one of the most robust principles in learning research. Gerbier and Toppino (2015) reviewed behavioural, neuroimaging and neurophysiological evidence and concluded that spaced practice and spaced testing are powerful tools for longer-lasting learning.

This supports the Trident-G design choice to avoid long grinding sessions. Short relational sessions repeated across days should be more compatible with slow integration than massed, fatigue-heavy training. It also supports delayed re-checks as measurement events: a relation that survives after time is more informative than a relation that only works inside the same training block.

### Consolidation and transfer/generalisation

There is also relevant evidence that consolidation affects **generalisation**. Censor (2013) reviewed perceptual and motor learning evidence and argued that generalisation can be affected by encoding and consolidation conditions. This is important for horizontal transfer, because the question is not simply whether a learner improves on a trained stimulus, but whether the trained relation remains usable under a changed surface or task wrapper.

For the Trident-G protocol, offline consolidation should become a formal design constraint:

```text
short relational session
→ controlled difficulty / wrapper perturbation
→ stop before fatigue or over-automation
→ sleep or spaced interval
→ delayed re-check
→ portability probe
→ puzzle / prompt / niche deployment
```

| Protocol feature                           | Consolidation rationale                                                                 |
| ------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Short sessions**                         | Reduce fatigue and prevent overfitting to a surface strategy.                           |
| **Every-other-day or spaced training**     | Allows offline stabilisation and integration between sessions.                          |
| **Sleep-sensitive scheduling**             | Treats good sleep as part of the learning protocol, not merely wellness advice.         |
| **Delayed probes**                         | Distinguish immediate task performance from retained, reusable structure.               |
| **Post-sleep / next-session return tests** | Test whether a relational rule or strategy reappears without same-block priming.        |
| **Wrapper swap after delay**               | Stronger test of whether consolidation supports abstraction beyond the trained surface. |
| **No endless same-day grinding**           | Avoids deepening brittle fast Gc before slow schematic extraction has occurred.         |

**Trident-G interpretation:** offline consolidation is a plausible and evidence-supported condition for durable learning, abstraction and some forms of insight. In the Trident-G protocol, sleep, spacing and delayed probes are design features that may support slow schematic Gc formation and generalisation, rather than optional recovery advice.

A compact summary is:

> Training builds candidate structure; sleep, spacing and delayed probes test whether it becomes reusable structure.

## Summary

The lower and middle layers of the Trident-G protocol are supported by converging but uneven evidence. MFT-M provides a validated model for estimating controlled evidence throughput and has shown selective transfer after training. Working-memory training reliably improves trained and structurally similar WM tasks, but ordinary WM training does not reliably produce broad far transfer. Relational binding and relational integration provide a stronger bridge from WM to Gf, because fluid reasoning depends heavily on maintaining and integrating relations rather than merely storing items. Wang et al. (2025) strengthen this argument by showing that relational integration training can modulate resting EEG microstate dynamics associated with frontoparietal networks for fluid intelligence. Strategy-enriched WM training, metacognitive instruction, self-explanation, inductive-reasoning training and implementation intentions provide evidence for the vertical-transfer principle: lower-level skills become more transferable when connected to explicit control policies and deployment scripts. Offline consolidation, spacing and sleep should be treated as part of the protocol because durable learning, abstraction and some forms of insight depend on post-training stabilisation and reorganisation. The horizontal-transfer principle — wrapper swaps, breakpoint perturbations and invariant recovery across surfaces — is theoretically coherent and supported indirectly by perceptual-learning transfer, piecewise learning curves and entropy–mutual-information learning dynamics, but it remains the least directly validated component. Therefore, Trident-G should be framed as a staged evidence-generating protocol rather than as an already proven far-transfer intervention.

# Eight meta-control skills for Trident-G transfer

## Overview

The Trident-G transfer model treats far transfer as more than improvement on a repeated task. The protocol aims to link state readiness, attention control, working-memory/SR-style inference, global-workspace prompts, implementation cues, real-world feedback and delayed re-use. In the source protocol, this is described as a chain in which **Zone trains the state**, **G trains the structure**, **prompts train the thinker**, **implementation intentions train deployment**, **wrapper swaps test horizontal transfer**, **niche probes test real-world coupling**, and **delayed probes test slow Gc**. 

On that basis, the eight-skill model can be treated as a compressed set of **language-accessible control policies** and **state–motivation regulators**. The first six are mainly cognitive, epistemic and strategic. The final two protect the affective, motivational and readiness conditions under which the first six can operate.

The strongest claim should remain evidence-bounded: the literature supports the components, but the full Trident-G stack is best described as an **evidence-generating far-transfer architecture**, not as an already proven g-raising intervention.

## Compressed review

### 1. Epistemic judgement and anomaly monitoring

Critical-thinking instruction provides the clearest evidence base for epistemic judgement. Abrami et al. (2015) define critical thinking as purposeful, self-regulatory judgement involving interpretation, analysis, evaluation, inference and explanation, and their meta-analysis found positive effects of instructional interventions on critical-thinking outcomes. This supports prompts that ask learners to evaluate evidence, test warrant and check whether an inference is valid (Abrami et al., 2015).  

This category can also absorb the **anomaly-monitoring** part of epistemic-emotion regulation. Surprise, curiosity and confusion are not merely distractions; they can signal violated expectations and motivate knowledge exploration when the learner does not collapse into avoidance or frustration. Vogl et al. (2020) found that epistemic emotions were linked to high-confidence errors and knowledge exploration, and the later replication/meta-analytic integration supported robust effects for epistemic emotions (Vogl et al., 2020).  

In Trident-G terms, this skill functions as a **model-error detector**: it helps the learner notice when the current representation is incomplete, inconsistent or overconfident.

### 2. Inductive / relational abstraction

Inductive-reasoning training gives one of the strongest precedents for training transferable relational abstraction. Klauer and Phye (2008) describe inductive reasoning as a comparison-based process involving regularities, irregularities and rule formation, and their review/meta-analysis summarises evidence from a large body of training experiments. This supports prompts such as “What is the same relation?”, “Which feature changed?”, and “What rule generalises?” (Klauer & Phye, 2008).  

This maps directly onto Trident-G’s claim that variable abstraction precedes SR-style inference. The learner must first identify the relevant dimension or relation before they can track transitions, consequences or reachable states.

### 3. Explanatory integration

Self-explanation is a strong candidate for converting local performance into deeper relational understanding. Bisra et al. (2018) define self-explanation as generating inferences about causal connections or conceptual relationships, and their meta-analysis included 69 effect sizes from 64 reports. This supports the use of prompts such as “Why did this move work?”, “What relation explains the result?”, and “What would the same explanation predict in a new case?” (Bisra et al., 2018). 

In Trident-G terms, explanatory integration helps prevent the learner from banking only a surface response. It pushes the learner towards a relational account that can be tested across wrappers and later recovered as slow schematic Gc.

### 4. Metacognitive strategy control

Metacognitive and self-regulated learning interventions provide broad evidence for training planning, monitoring and revision. Donker et al. (2014) reviewed 58 studies of cognitive, metacognitive and management strategy instruction in primary and secondary education and found that learning-strategy instruction improved academic performance. Dignath et al. (2008) similarly found positive effects of self-regulated learning interventions on learning outcomes, strategy use and motivation in primary-school samples (Dignath et al., 2008; Donker et al., 2014). 

This skill is about regulating the learner’s strategy use rather than directly solving the problem. Its core questions are: “What is my plan?”, “Is this working?”, “What evidence says I should continue?”, and “Should I switch strategy?”

### 5. Strategic problem-space control

Strategic problem-space control is narrower than metacognition. It concerns the structure of the problem itself: current state, goal state, operators, constraints, subgoals, dead ends and next tests. Newell and Simon’s problem-space theory remains the classic foundation: a task environment is represented by the solver as a problem space, and the structure of that problem space determines the possible programmes or strategies for solving it (Newell & Simon, 1972).  

The intervention evidence is strongest when problem-space control is operationalised as schema or strategy instruction rather than vague “strategic thinking”. Peltier and Vannest’s meta-analysis of schema instruction included 21 studies with 3,408 elementary-school participants and found large effects for immediate and transfer problem-solving outcomes in mathematics (Peltier & Vannest, 2017).  

For IQ Coach, this supports using bounded puzzle spaces as application tasks after WM/CCC training. The source notes make the same point: puzzles instantiate constrained problem spaces, while prompts make the epistemic policy portable and implementation intentions/missions test deployment.

## 6. Implementation deployment and real-world missions

Implementation intentions are the clearest evidence base for moving from strategy possession to strategy deployment. Gollwitzer and Sheeran’s meta-analysis found that if–then plans had a medium-to-large effect on goal attainment, supporting the idea that cue-linked plans help people initiate action, protect goal pursuit and act under real-world conditions (Gollwitzer & Sheeran, 2006). More recent meta-analytic evidence on mental contrasting with implementation intentions also supports the value of pairing desired outcomes with anticipated obstacles and cue-linked action plans (Wang et al., 2021).

In Trident-G terms, implementation intentions should not be treated as reasoning skills. They are **deployment scripts**. They help a trained control policy fire in the right context.

A simple in-app version is:

```text
If I am stuck for 30 seconds,
then I will identify the current state, goal, constraint and next test.
```

A stronger mission version is:

```text
If I face a confusing real-world problem today,
then I will write:
state → goal → constraints → next test,
take one small action,
and record what happened.
```

The mission is not just homework. It is the **niche-coupling test**. It asks whether the trained policy activates under live cues, survives ordinary noise and produces informative feedback. In Trident-G language, this is where vertical transfer becomes niche transfer: the learner is not merely able to reconstruct a control policy in the app, but can deploy it in the environment that gives the policy meaning. This is consistent with transfer-of-training evidence showing that transfer depends not only on training content, but also on trainee factors, intervention design and the environment in which the trained skill must be used (Blume et al., 2010).

Real-world missions can be classified into a small set of recurring transfer contexts. Most missions will involve **comprehension and explanation**: making sense of a situation, identifying the relevant variables, and explaining why something is happening. A second class involves **argumentation and epistemic judgement**: evaluating evidence, testing claims, identifying assumptions, and asking what would make a conclusion wrong. A third class involves **strategic action**: defining the current state, goal, constraints, operators and next test before taking a concrete step. A fourth class involves **learning and skill acquisition**: using metacognitive strategies to plan, monitor, evaluate and revise one’s learning. A fifth class, especially relevant for resilience applications, involves **appraisal and self-regulation**: detecting emotional or bodily cues, separating sensation from prediction, choosing an adaptive probe, and updating the appraisal model from outcome feedback. These categories fit the Trident-G mission logic because each one turns an in-app mindware script into a cue-triggered real-world action that can generate feedback for vertical credit assignment and delayed reuse (Abrami et al., 2015; Dignath et al., 2008; Gollwitzer & Sheeran, 2006; Hattie & Donoghue, 2016; Hattie & Timperley, 2007). The Trident-G protocol already frames this as the move from in-app control-policy reconstruction to real-world niche coupling, where a trained policy must activate under live cues, guide useful action, generate informative feedback and survive delayed reuse. 

A compact classification table could sit underneath:

| Mission family                               | Core question                                                 | Example mission                                                           |
| -------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Comprehension / explanation**              | “What is happening, and why?”                                 | Explain a confusing work problem using variables, causes and constraints. |
| **Argumentation / epistemic judgement**      | “What is the evidence, and what would make this wrong?”       | Test a strong claim by listing assumptions and counterevidence.           |
| **Strategic action / problem-space control** | “What is the state, goal, constraint and next test?”          | Convert a stuck task into one concrete next action.                       |
| **Learning / skill acquisition**             | “How should I plan, monitor and revise my learning?”          | Use a metacognitive learning loop on a new concept or skill.              |
| **Appraisal / self-regulation**              | “What am I predicting, and what small probe could update it?” | Reframe a threat cue through sensation → prediction → action → outcome.   |

> **Missions are not generic homework tasks. They are real-world transfer probes classified by the kind of cognitive policy being deployed: explanation, argumentation, strategic action, learning, or appraisal regulation.**

The mission outcome then becomes reinforcement for the vertical stack. Feedback should not merely reward completion. Effective feedback should help the learner understand the goal, their current progress and the next action to take (Hattie & Timperley, 2007). This is important because feedback interventions can improve performance, but they can also be ineffective or counterproductive when they direct attention away from the task or process level (Kluger & DeNisi, 1996).

This creates **vertical credit assignment**. The system can reinforce accurate cue detection, correct variable selection, useful prompt use, effective action, good outcome logging and later reuse. At a mechanistic level, this is compatible with reinforcement-learning accounts in which prediction-error signals help update policies when outcomes are better or worse than expected (Schultz et al., 1997; Schultz, 2016). The Trident-G protocol already frames this as the reinforced chain linking state readiness, attention control, variable binding, SR-style inference, epistemic prompt selection, mission action, real-world implementation and delayed reuse.

| Trained layer             | Mission cue                                       | Mission action                                              | Reinforcement signal                    |

**Implementation intentions have strong evidence for cue-linked goal deployment (Gollwitzer & Sheeran, 2006). Real-world missions are a theoretically coherent extension because they convert trained mindware scripts into cue-triggered actions that generate environmental feedback. This supports the Trident-G vertical-transfer and niche-transfer logic, but the full mission-based reinforcement stack still needs direct validation.**

The compact protocol wording would be:

```text
Implementation intentions train deployment.
Missions test niche coupling.
Outcome feedback reinforces the vertical chain.
Delayed reuse tests whether the policy consolidated.
```

### 7. Zone and epistemic-emotion regulation

Zone regulation adds the state-readiness layer: the learner must be sufficiently engaged, stable and challenged to use the cognitive skills above. Flow research supports part of this claim. Fong et al. (2015) meta-analysed 28 studies on challenge–skill balance, flow and intrinsic motivation, finding a moderate relation between challenge–skill balance and flow. Harris et al. (2021/2023) reviewed and meta-analysed evidence on flow–performance relations, while also noting that the strength and quality of evidence vary by domain and method (Fong et al., 2015; Harris et al., 2023).  

The safe interpretation is not “flow can be trained on demand”. It is that adaptive learning benefits from keeping challenge and skill in a workable band and from using signs of surprise, confusion or overload as regulatory cues. This fits the Trident-G role of Zone: state readiness gates deeper training, while prompts and task difficulty should be routed according to whether the learner is ready to push, stabilise, widen or recover. 

### 8. Motivational agency and resilient engagement

The final category compresses goal–value alignment, goal focus, self-efficacy and persistence. Self-determination theory provides strong support for the importance of motivational quality: Howard et al. (2021) found that different types of motivation, ranging from external incentives to personal value and intrinsic interest, relate differently to student outcomes. In protocol terms, this supports linking tasks to personally meaningful value rather than relying only on external reward or gamified points (Howard et al., 2021).  

Goal-setting theory provides a second strong strand. Locke and Latham’s work shows that specific, difficult goals generally produce better performance than vague “do your best” goals, although effects can be moderated by task complexity and the learner’s ability to discover appropriate strategies (Locke & Latham, 2002). 

Self-efficacy adds the agency component. Multon et al. (1991) found positive relations between self-efficacy beliefs and academic performance/persistence across a wide range of contexts. Persistence should be included, but not overclaimed: Credé et al.’s meta-analysis suggests that broad claims about grit are overstated, and that perseverance of effort is more useful than the broader grit construct, which overlaps substantially with conscientiousness (Credé et al., 2017; Multon et al., 1991).  

So the practical Trident-G category should be **motivational agency and resilient engagement**, not “grit training”. It asks whether the learner has enough value, reward salience, goal clarity, perceived control and adaptive persistence to remain engaged without becoming rigid.

## Synthesis for the protocol

The eight skills can be organised into four functional pairs:

| Pair                                     | Skills | Function                                                                        |
| ---------------------------------------- | ------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Truth and pattern control**            | 1–2    | Evaluate evidence and abstract the invariant relation.                          |
| **Meaning and strategy control**         | 3–4    | Explain why the relation works and monitor whether the strategy is working.     |
| **Problem-space and deployment control** | 5–6    | Navigate the structured task space and trigger the strategy in the right niche. |
| **State and motivation control**         | 7–8    | Stay in the workable training band and maintain meaningful, agentic engagement. |

This gives a compact model of human-led intelligence training:

```text
evidence → relation → explanation → strategy → problem space → deployment → zone → agency
```

The scientific claim :

> The literature supports each component as a plausible contributor to transfer-relevant learning, strategy use, goal-directed behaviour or performance regulation. The novel Trident-G claim is that these components become more powerful when integrated into a vertical transfer chain that links state, attention, relational workspace, problem-space application, prompt selection, implementation, feedback and delayed re-use.

That is aligned with the source protocol’s claim boundary: the architecture is empirically motivated and theoretically coherent, but the full stack still needs staged validation against active controls and delayed transfer outcomes.
