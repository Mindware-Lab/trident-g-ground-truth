The four SR–inference families could be adapted into a **transdiagnostic cognitive-restructuring architecture**, with disorder-specific content and behavioural tests.

The strongest claim is not that these tasks would themselves constitute CBT. Rather:

> **They could provide an instrumented mechanism layer for assessing, practising and transferring the inference operations used within CBT and related psychological interventions.**

CBT already works partly by identifying relationships among situations, thoughts, beliefs, emotions and behaviour; testing interpretations; and using behavioural experience to revise predictions. Cognitive restructuring is associated with psychotherapy outcomes, while behavioural experiments allow beliefs to be tested against real-world evidence. ([PMC][1])

## 1. The four clinical inference families

The original four families translate naturally into four therapeutic operations:

| SR/inference family               | Clinical adaptation                             | Core therapeutic question                                                      |
| --------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Order and chains**              | Maintenance-chain inference                     | What sequence keeps this problem going?                                        |
| **Transformations and analogies** | Reappraisal and perspective transformation      | What alternative interpretation preserves the facts but changes their meaning? |
| **Rules and constraints**         | Conditional-belief and boundary inference       | What rule am I applying, and where does it actually hold?                      |
| **Prediction and strategy**       | Behavioural experimentation and adaptive action | What do I predict, and what action would test or update that prediction?       |

The invariant architecture would remain:

```text
signal–noise discrimination
→ accumulated evidence and confidence updating
→ calibrated stopping or action threshold
→ behavioural test
→ prediction error
→ revised and banked schema
```

This is consistent with Marković et al.’s claim that adaptive control requires inference over both external contexts and internal meta-control modes—particularly whether more information seeking or more instrumental action is appropriate.

# 2. Order and chains: maintenance-model restructuring

This family would represent the sequence connecting a trigger to the continuation of symptoms.

A generic CBT chain might be:

```text
situation
→ attention capture
→ interpretation
→ emotion/body response
→ safety behaviour or avoidance
→ short-term consequence
→ long-term maintenance
```

### Signal–noise component

The person identifies the most causally relevant link amid plausible but secondary details.

For example:

* Was the distress caused directly by the event, or by its interpretation?
* Did avoidance reduce actual danger, or merely reduce anxiety temporarily?
* Which part of the sequence is maintaining the cycle?

### Accumulation component

Information is revealed across several examples or episodes. The person gradually learns which links recur and updates confidence in the maintenance model.

### Pressure/stopping component

The person must stop elaborating the chain once enough has been identified to select an intervention.

This guards against two poles:

```text
Fast brittle:
“I felt anxious, therefore the situation was dangerous.”

Slow compensatory:
continuing to analyse every historical cause
after the current maintenance link is already actionable.
```

### Disorder-specific examples

| Presentation       | Example maintenance chain                                                                                            |
| ------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Depression**     | setback → global negative appraisal → withdrawal → reduced reinforcement → stronger negative appraisal               |
| **Social anxiety** | social cue → threat interpretation → self-focused attention → safety behaviour → ambiguous outcome → belief retained |
| **OCD**            | intrusion → catastrophic responsibility appraisal → compulsion → short-term relief → increased future salience       |
| **PTSD**           | reminder → “danger is happening now” appraisal → arousal/avoidance → failure to update present safety                |

These adaptations are broadly compatible with established CBT formulations, but the treatment context matters. NICE recommends structured CBT for depression and disorder-specific CBT involving cognitive restructuring and exposure for social anxiety. ([NICE][2])

# 3. Transformations and analogies: reappraisal

The transformation family asks whether the same evidence can support a different, more proportionate representation.

The relevant operator is not:

```text
negative thought → positive thought
```

It is closer to:

```text
initial appraisal
→ preserve the observable facts
→ alter an unsupported inference
→ derive a more balanced appraisal
```

### Signal–noise component

Discriminate genuine reappraisal from:

* denial;
* reassurance;
* implausible positivity;
* simple word replacement;
* an alternative that ignores important evidence.

### Accumulation component

Successive pieces of evidence support or weaken candidate interpretations.

For example:

```text
“They did not reply.”
→ “They dislike me.”
→ alternative causes considered
→ base-rate/context evidence added
→ confidence redistributed across explanations
```

### Pressure/stopping component

Select a sufficiently credible alternative without either accepting the first comforting explanation or generating endless alternatives.

```text
Fast brittle:
select the first salient interpretation.

Slow compensatory:
generate fifteen interpretations and still refuse provisional closure.

Regulated:
retain several possibilities,
weight them by evidence,
and act on the best current model.
```

This could be useful for depression, anxiety, social threat interpretation and perfectionistic appraisals. A social-anxiety trial using computational modelling found measurable differences in evidence accumulation, illustrating that clinically relevant biases can potentially be decomposed into evidence uptake and decision parameters rather than inferred from accuracy alone. ([PMC][3])

# 4. Rules and constraints: conditional beliefs

This may be the most direct bridge to cognitive restructuring.

Common CBT beliefs often have a rule-like structure:

```text
If I make a mistake, I will be rejected.

Unless I feel completely certain, I must keep checking.

If anxiety is present, danger must be present.

I must perform perfectly in order to be acceptable.
```

### Signal–noise component

Identify the actual rule rather than surrounding emotional or autobiographical detail.

### Accumulation component

Infer the rule’s boundary conditions from:

* confirming examples;
* exceptions;
* counterexamples;
* context shifts;
* near-miss cases.

### Pressure/stopping component

Decide when the evidence is sufficient to revise the rule provisionally.

The task should avoid two opposite errors:

```text
Underconstraint:
one counterexample is treated as proving the belief completely false.

Overconstraint:
every counterexample generates another qualification,
allowing the belief to become unfalsifiable.
```

A clinically useful output would be:

```text
Old rule:
“If I make an error, others will lose respect for me.”

Revised rule:
“Some errors affect trust when they are serious or concealed,
but ordinary acknowledged errors usually do not determine overall respect.”

Behavioural boundary test:
Make or disclose one minor, safe imperfection and observe the response.
```

This is a Trident-G portability cascade:

```text
identify rule
→ test near-miss
→ test boundary
→ change context
→ behavioural experiment
→ delayed re-check
→ bank only what survives
```

# 5. Prediction and strategy: behavioural experiments

The fourth family would turn cognitive restructuring into **active prediction testing**.

The person specifies:

```text
What do I think will happen?
How confident am I?
What observable result would count?
What is the smallest safe test?
What happened?
How much should the prediction now change?
```

### Signal–noise component

Distinguish:

* possible from probable;
* threatening from informative;
* rare from impossible;
* short-term relief from long-term improvement;
* actual outcomes from post-hoc reinterpretation.

### Accumulation component

Update outcome probabilities across repeated behavioural experiments rather than treating one experience as definitive.

### Pressure/stopping component

Decide when to stop seeking certainty and perform the test.

This is particularly close to Marković’s explore–exploit formulation:

```text
continue modelling or reassurance seeking
versus
take an informative external action
```

In Trident-G terms, behavioural experiments restore **λ**, because the belief is coupled to external feedback rather than being debated entirely within the internal model.

# 6. How the four Zone profiles would appear clinically

| Zone-Cog profile        | Cognitive-restructuring expression                                                                                                   |
| ----------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Regulated**           | Identifies the relevant appraisal, considers credible alternatives, updates confidence and performs a proportionate behavioural test |
| **Slow compensatory**   | Understands the formulation but overanalyses, seeks excessive evidence, repeatedly qualifies alternatives or delays the experiment   |
| **Globally overloaded** | Cannot separate trigger, thought, emotion and outcome; evidence becomes fragmented; restructuring adds cognitive burden              |
| **Fast brittle**        | Accepts the first interpretation, jumps to a conclusion, applies a global rule or acts before sufficient evidence has accumulated    |

The **jumping-to-conclusions** literature offers an established example of the fast-brittle pole. In probabilistic reasoning tasks, people with delusions have, on average, sometimes made decisions after fewer observations, although the construct is heterogeneous and task measurement has limitations. ([PMC][4])

The opposite clinical pole would be:

```text
excessive information gathering
→ reassurance
→ checking
→ rumination
→ inability to declare evidence sufficient
```

The important therapeutic target is therefore neither maximum reflection nor maximum decisiveness. It is **context-calibrated evidence collection and commitment**.

# 7. Different disorders require different clinical wrappers

The same computational core should not be delivered identically across conditions.

## Depression

Likely targets:

* negative causal chains;
* global and stable inferential rules;
* underweighting of positive or corrective evidence;
* pessimistic action predictions;
* withdrawal that prevents model updating.

The fourth family should connect closely to behavioural activation rather than remaining purely verbal. NICE describes CBT for depression as structured, goal-oriented and focused on relationships among thoughts, beliefs, feelings and behaviour. ([NICE][2])

## Anxiety and social anxiety

Likely targets:

* threat-signal selection;
* ambiguous interpretation;
* safety rules;
* overestimation of probability or cost;
* avoidance that prevents prediction-error learning.

The prediction/strategy layer would culminate in graded behavioural experiments or exposure, not simply alternative thoughts. NICE’s social-anxiety CBT specification explicitly combines cognitive restructuring, core-belief modification and graduated exposure. ([NICE][5])

## OCD

Likely targets:

* inflated responsibility;
* thought–action fusion;
* certainty rules;
* excessive evidence accumulation and checking;
* failure to tolerate unresolved uncertainty.

However, the task must not become another reassurance or checking procedure. For OCD, the clinical bridge should normally support **exposure and response prevention**, not endlessly evaluate whether the obsession is true. NICE recommends CBT including ERP, with exposure to obsessive thoughts and prevention of overt or mental neutralising responses. ([NICE][6])

## PTSD

Likely targets:

* temporal-order/context confusion;
* “then versus now” discrimination;
* overgeneralised danger rules;
* trauma-related meanings;
* avoidance and threat prediction.

This would need to be embedded within trauma-focused treatment. NICE recommends individual trauma-focused CBT approaches such as cognitive processing therapy, cognitive therapy for PTSD, narrative exposure therapy and prolonged exposure. ([NICE][7])

## Psychosis or paranoia

Likely targets:

* jumping to conclusions;
* source/context binding;
* alternative-cause generation;
* belief flexibility;
* confidence calibration.

These tasks would need especially careful, collaborative delivery rather than confrontational belief disputation. NICE recommends disorder-specific CBT for psychosis, delivered by trained professionals. ([NICE][8])

# 8. Best product/research architecture

I would use four stages:

```text
1. Mechanism probe
Estimate whether the bottleneck is signal selection,
evidence accumulation, confidence updating or stopping control.

2. Neutral training
Train the operation first using emotionally neutral graph material.

3. Clinically relevant wrapper
Transfer the same operation to carefully graded,
condition-relevant scenarios.

4. Real-world behavioural experiment
Use a therapist-approved action, record predictions and outcomes,
and conduct a delayed re-check.
```

This follows your horizontal-transfer principle:

```text
neutral invariant
→ changed emotional wrapper
→ expected performance dip
→ supported recovery
→ mixed presentation
→ real-world deployment
→ delayed re-check
```

It also protects against teaching only a disorder-specific verbal script. The aim would be to train the **inference policy** and then test whether it remains accessible under affective load.

## Essential qualification

The proposed **four-family × three-process system is currently a theoretical and research architecture**, not a validated treatment. Existing evidence supports CBT, cognitive restructuring, behavioural experiments and disorder-specific procedures, but it does not yet establish that this exact SR-based training stack improves symptoms or clinical functioning.

Accordingly, it should initially be positioned as:

* an experimental process measure;
* a therapist-facing formulation aid;
* an adjunctive cognitive exercise;
* a behavioural-experiment preparation tool;
* a transfer-sensitive research platform.

It should not initially be positioned as:

* a diagnostic system;
* an autonomous therapist;
* a replacement for manualised CBT, ERP or trauma-focused treatment;
* a validated selector of treatment;
* evidence of symptom improvement based solely on task performance.

For higher-risk conditions and clinically focused digital interventions, trained-practitioner support, monitoring of progress and risk, and clear escalation pathways are important; NICE’s digitally enabled therapy recommendations similarly retain trained support and monitoring rather than treating the software as autonomous care. ([NICE][9])

## Distilled formulation

> **The four SR families can become four cognitive-restructuring operations: reconstruct the maintenance chain, transform the appraisal, revise the conditional rule and test the future prediction. At each level, signal selection, evidence accumulation and stopping control determine whether restructuring is regulated, slow-compensatory, overloaded or fast-brittle.**

The complete clinical G-loop would be:

```text
detect the relevant cue
→ reconstruct the maintenance chain
→ generate a credible transformation
→ identify the governing rule and its boundary
→ state the prediction
→ select a safe behavioural test
→ update confidence from the outcome
→ repeat across contexts
→ bank only the restructuring policy that survives
```
